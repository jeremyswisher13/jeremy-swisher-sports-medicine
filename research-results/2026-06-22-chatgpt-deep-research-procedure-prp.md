JSM Procedure Evidence Matrix + PRP Peri-Procedure Plans
Scope and evidence frame

This review synthesizes current guideline statements, systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and key randomized trials for six common sports medicine procedures across the condition list you provided. I used the requested prioritization where possible, leaning first on AAOS, ACR/Arthritis Foundation, NICE, AMSSM, JOSPT/APTA clinical practice guidelines, Cochrane reviews, and higher-level reviews or RCTs when guideline coverage was incomplete. 
1

In this matrix, commonly discussed means the procedure is a standard or frequently considered option in routine sports medicine discussions for that condition; selected cases means the procedure is reasonable in narrower scenarios, usually after failed conservative care or when anatomy/diagnostic uncertainty matters; rarely means it is not routine and usually has limited, conflicting, or niche evidence; not indicated means current evidence or guideline direction does not support routine use for that condition in a patient-facing sports medicine app. A “commonly discussed” label does not mean “first-line,” and a “selected cases” label does not mean “ineffective.” 
2

Major cross-cutting themes are consistent across the evidence base: exercise and load management remain foundational for most tendinopathies; corticosteroid injections tend to have their clearest role in short-term symptom relief rather than tissue healing; PRP is most plausible for some chronic tendinopathies and knee OA but remains heterogeneous by indication and preparation; hyaluronic acid is now weak or negative in several major OA guidelines; ESWT is strongest for plantar fasciopathy and some chronic tendinopathies, with focused-vs-radial nuance depending on tissue depth and calcification; and percutaneous needle/ultrasonic tenotomy is generally reserved for refractory cases and supported more by observational than randomized evidence.

Procedure-specific cautions, contraindications, and rehab implications

Ultrasound-guided procedures. The main advantage is better targeting rather than a different medication effect; evidence is strongest for accuracy and short-term outcome gains in several shoulder and deep-joint applications. Cautions are practical and anatomic: image guidance does not make an otherwise poor indication appropriate, and it becomes most useful when the target is small, deep, adjacent to neurovascular structures, or when prior blind injection failed. Rehab implications are usually minimal and depend more on the injectate or procedure performed than on the ultrasound itself. 
4

Corticosteroid injection. Key contraindications and cautions include overlying skin infection or cellulitis, septic arthritis or suspected joint infection, bacteremia or sepsis, unstable fracture or gross instability, and caution in people with diabetes, recent or planned arthroplasty, repeated injections into the same structure, or tendon-pathology situations where tissue weakening matters. A 2025 multisociety corticosteroid guideline emphasizes short-term benefit but also notes risks including blood-glucose elevation, adrenal suppression, cartilage concerns, bone effects, and postoperative joint infection; an ASRA preprocedural document recommends avoiding intra-articular steroid injection within one month of planned replacement surgery in that same joint. Post-procedure rehab should avoid using the temporary pain relief to overload the tissue too early, which is especially important for tendons and the plantar fascia. 
53

PRP. PRP is best framed as an evolving orthobiologic rather than a guaranteed regenerative treatment. Common clinician cautions include active infection, bleeding-risk questions, major blood-count abnormalities, and medication issues that may alter platelet function or make venipuncture/injection unsafe; AMSSM’s position statement emphasizes responsible use, patient selection, and transparent discussion of uncertainty. Rehab implications matter more than with standard cortisone: most clinics deliberately use relative rest first, then staged re-loading, because post-PRP soreness is expected and the procedure is usually paired with a tendon- or joint-specific rehabilitation plan rather than “rest until it feels better.” 
54

Hyaluronic acid injection. The main caution is not procedural danger so much as indication drift: major OA guidelines now recommend against routine knee HA use and strongly against hip HA because benefit over placebo is small or absent in current evidence summaries. When it is still considered, it tends to be because a patient is trying to avoid corticosteroid or has had partial benefit previously, not because the evidence base is especially strong. Rehab implications are light; patients usually return quickly to baseline activity while continuing exercise-based OA care. 
5

Extracorporeal shockwave therapy. ESWT is noninvasive, but it is still a procedure and should be matched to the right diagnosis and tissue depth. The best-supported applications in this set are plantar fasciopathy, gluteal tendinopathy/GTPS, some Achilles and proximal hamstring cases, and calcific rotator cuff disease; for noncalcific rotator cuff tendinopathy, the 2025 shoulder CPG recommends against it. Focused ESWT is generally the evidence-backed choice when a deeper target or calcific focus is the goal, while radial pressure wave therapy is often used more superficially and in broader “pressure wave” protocols. Rehab implications are important: ISMST guidance emphasizes load adaptation and sport modification after treatment rather than treating ESWT as a stand-alone cure. 
55

Percutaneous needle or ultrasonic tenotomy. This is usually a refractory-tendinopathy procedure, not an early treatment. It is best supported for selected chronic tendon conditions such as lateral elbow, gluteal, Achilles, patellar, plantar fascia, and other long-standing tendinopathies after failed loading-based care, but the overall evidence quality remains limited because most studies are case series or small cohorts. The rehab implication is the biggest practical point: unlike steroid injections, these procedures usually require a more protective early phase and a slower return to heavy loading, because the intent is tissue debridement/fenestration followed by structured remodeling. 
19

Where the guidelines and expert groups conflict

The biggest conflict is PRP for knee OA. AAOS says PRP may reduce pain and improve function, but only with limited strength of recommendation. ACR/Arthritis Foundation recommends against PRP for knee and hip OA, whereas the newly released 2026 AAPM&R guidance recommends considering PRP for mild-to-moderate knee OA after failed conservative care. That is not a minor wording difference; it reflects a real split between a conservative rheumatology guideline lens and a procedure-oriented musculoskeletal/PM&R lens. 
5

There is also a long-standing conflict around hyaluronic acid for knee OA. AAOS recommends against routine use, and ACR/Arthritis Foundation also recommends against it, while older AMSSM viscosupplementation statements were more favorable about individualized use and payer coverage implications. In practice, this means HA remains widely discussed even though the guideline trend is more negative than it was a decade ago. 
5

For chronic spine pain, the divergence is especially sharp. The 2025 BMJ Rapid Recommendation strongly recommends against common interventional procedures for chronic axial and chronic radicular spine pain, including epidural steroid injections, while interventional pain societies publicly criticized that guideline as methodologically flawed and older spine/pain guidance plus some coverage policies remain more permissive for selected radicular cases. For a patient-facing sports medicine app, the safest summary is that simple mechanical neck and low-back pain are poor procedure targets, while radiculopathy remains a specialist-selected rather than routine primary-care sports medicine procedure. 
50

For Achilles tendinopathy, historical caution about corticosteroid due to tendon risk now sits alongside a 2022 randomized trial showing benefit when steroid injection was paired with exercise therapy. That does not erase the tissue-risk concern, but it does mean “never inject Achilles-related pain” is now too simplistic. 
43

For rotator cuff tendinopathy, ESWT is another nuance point. Broad statements that “shockwave works for shoulder tendinopathy” are misleading; the current shoulder CPG recommends against ESWT for noncalcific rotator cuff tendinopathy while allowing it for calcific disease. 
22

PRP planning for patients and clinicians
Pre-PRP checklist

Activity and exercise before injection. For chronic tendon problems, the strongest evidence-based principle is not complete rest but load management. The Achilles CPG explicitly advises that complete rest is not indicated and that tendon-loading exercise remains first-line care; most PRP practices therefore reduce provocative loading rather than stopping all movement. A practical patient-facing version is: keep normal daily walking and gentle range of motion, but scale down the activities that reliably flare the tendon or joint in the several days before the injection. For heavy eccentrics, plyometrics, sprinting, maximal gripping, or heavy overhead lifting, temporary reduction is mostly consensus/common protocol rather than directly PRP-trial-based evidence. 
56

Whether to continue rehab. Continue nonprovocative rehab and mobility work unless the treating clinician says otherwise. For tendon indications, isometrics, controlled mobility, and low-irritability strengthening are usually reasonable right up to the procedure, while very high-load or flare-producing sessions are commonly reduced for 24 to 72 hours before treatment. This is mostly expert-informed / consensus rather than standardized across trials. 
56

Medication considerations before PRP. The best-supported medication issue is platelet-active analgesics. A 2022 systematic review concluded that aspirin, acetaminophen, and nonselective NSAIDs should be considered for suspension before PRP because of their potential to diminish platelet effects, while COX-2–selective NSAIDs and statins did not need to be withheld based on available data. A 2024 review further suggested that stronger COX-1 NSAIDs deserve more caution than COX-2–selective drugs. That said, medication decisions should be coordinated with the treating clinician, and safety outweighs theoretical PRP optimization. 
57

Supplements and lifestyle. There is not good evidence that supplements meaningfully “improve PRP quality.” Advice to stop fish oil, turmeric, garlic, ginkgo, or vitamin E is mostly consensus/common protocol or theoretical/biologic rationale, not something supported by strong PRP outcome data. Sleep, hydration, and adequate protein/calorie intake are sensible general peri-procedure advice, but direct PRP-specific outcome evidence is limited. Smoking/nicotine reduction is reasonable because smoking is associated with worse tendon and soft-tissue health generally, but there is little PRP-specific peri-injection evidence. 
58

When the patient should contact the clinician before PRP. Contact the treating office before the procedure if there is fever, viral illness, skin infection near the site, a new antibiotic course, a new blood thinner or aspirin change, abnormal bruising/bleeding, pregnancy questions, a recent steroid injection into the same region, or inability to safely follow the post-procedure restrictions. Those are mostly safety-driven physician decisions rather than PRP efficacy predictors.

Post-PRP recovery by condition cluster

Tendon conditions. A reasonable patient-facing explanation is that tendons often feel more irritated for several days after PRP, then gradually shift into a structured re-loading phase. The most evidence-based part of tendon recovery is not the PRP-specific timeline itself but the principle that progressive tendon loading remains the backbone of recovery in Achilles, patellar, rotator cuff, and other tendinopathies. What is evidence-based is the importance of staged loading; what is mostly expert-informed is the exact day-by-day PRP schedule, because trials use different injection preparations and rehab protocols. 
60

Joint/OA conditions. For knee or hip OA, the early post-PRP period is usually shorter and less protective than tendon PRP. Most clinicians use relative rest for a couple of days, then resume range of motion, walking, and a graded OA exercise program fairly quickly. The evidence base supports exercise therapy as core OA care, but there is no universal PRP-aftercare schedule endorsed across AAOS, ACR, or AAPM&R. 
61

Hand/wrist tendon sheath conditions. If PRP is discussed at all for de Quervain or trigger finger, it should be framed as niche and nonstandard. Recovery tends to be shorter than for a major lower-extremity tendon, with brief protection followed by range of motion and gentle hand use, but this is mostly expert-informed because the evidence base is too limited to justify a universal protocol.

Suggested staged PRP rehab timelines

These timelines are intentionally conservative and not a claim that one universal PRP protocol exists.

Tendon PRP timeline.
Immediate post-procedure period: relative rest, protected walking or arm use as needed, and gentle range of motion if allowed. This is mostly expert-informed. 
54

Early protection / relative rest: usually several days to about 1 to 2 weeks depending on tendon and whether needling/tenotomy was also performed; avoid sprinting, jumping, maximal gripping, or heavy overhead loading. This is mostly consensus/common protocol. 
39

Reintroduction of ROM and isometrics: often begins once resting pain begins to settle; isometrics and controlled mobility are commonly used first. This is probably best viewed as expert-informed built on tendinopathy rehab principles. 
60

Progressive strengthening: usually follows as symptoms allow, then heavy-slow resistance or eccentric loading if that fits the tendon involved. The strongest evidence here comes from tendinopathy rehab literature, not PRP-specific trials. 
60

Running, jumping, gripping, overhead, or sport-specific return: should be milestone-based rather than calendar-based, using pain response, tolerance of progressive load, and function tests. This is mostly expert-informed. 
62

Joint/OA PRP timeline.
Immediate post-procedure period: relative rest and walking/activity reduction for a short period, usually days rather than weeks. Consensus/common protocol. 
63

Early reintroduction: resume gait, ROM, cycling, pool work, and standard OA exercise progressions as pain allows. Evidence-based insofar as exercise remains core OA care. 
5

Progressive strengthening and impact return: advance based on pain irritability, swelling, and function rather than assuming PRP alone will “fix” the joint. Expert-informed. 
63

Warning signs after PRP

Patients should contact the treating team urgently for fever, spreading redness, drainage, rapidly increasing swelling, severe pain that is clearly worse than the expected early flare, new numbness or weakness, or hand/foot color changes. Calf swelling, shortness of breath, or chest pain require urgent medical evaluation because those symptoms raise concern for clot or cardiopulmonary complications rather than an ordinary post-injection flare. These are general post-procedure safety rules, not PRP-specific claims that such events are common.

Concise patient-facing content blocks

What PRP is. “PRP uses a concentrated sample of your own blood to deliver platelets to a painful tendon or joint. In some conditions it may improve pain and function, but results vary by diagnosis and PRP preparation, and it is not guaranteed to regenerate tissue.” 
54

What PRP is not. “PRP is not a shortcut around rehab. For most tendon and joint conditions, the exercise and loading plan still matters as much as the injection.” 
60

What to expect after PRP. “A short pain flare is common. Most clinicians use a brief relative-rest period and then a gradual return to range of motion and strengthening instead of complete rest.” 
54

Clinician-facing caveats

PRP evidence is highly indication-specific. Knee OA and lateral epicondylitis have much stronger “commonly discussed” footing than Achilles tendinopathy, trigger finger, TFCC pain, or labral-type hip pain. Do not export a single PRP promise statement across all body regions. 
64

For ESWT, store a calcific vs noncalcific flag for rotator cuff conditions and a focused vs radial attribute for deeper tendon targets such as gluteal and proximal hamstring disease. 
55

For corticosteroid injections, include a “short-term symptom relief” caveat by default for OA and tendinopathy content. Avoid language that implies disease healing, especially for degenerative tendon disease. 
65

Open questions and limitations

Some conditions in your list have materially thinner high-level evidence than others, especially medial epicondylitis, isolated biceps tendinopathy, TFCC pain, trigger finger PRP, and de Quervain PRP. For those cells, I kept the matrix conservative and distinguished clearer evidence from practice patterns. 
20

I also separated evidence-based PRP rehabilitation principles from expert-informed protocol details because the literature does not support one universal hold-list for every medication, one universal supplement rule, or one universal post-PRP loading schedule across all tissues and PRP preparations. 
54
